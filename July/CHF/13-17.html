CHF Deep-Dive Fundamental Report

As of: 2026-07-16  |  Scope: Single-currency deep dive on CHF — SNB policy at the zero bound, inflation, growth, the US tariff overhang, safe-haven mechanics, positioning, cross-by-cross bias, catalysts & scenario tree  |  Companion piece to: Daily Global Macro Strategy Report / EUR Deep-Dive

One-line view: CHF is the market's cleanest structural haven trade layered on top of a central bank that has almost no conventional ammunition left — the SNB is pinned at a 0% policy rate, inflation is barely positive, and its main active tool right now is FX intervention rhetoric rather than the interest-rate lever. That combination makes CHF's near-term path less about Swiss data surprises and much more about (1) how the Iran war headlines move global risk appetite and (2) whether the SNB's patience with a strong franc runs out. A second, franc-specific thread — the still-unresolved US tariff situation — is a real and somewhat underappreciated swing factor that doesn't exist for EUR in the same form.

1. What's Actually Driving CHF Right Now — The Core Tension

CHF today is being shaped by three forces that don't all point the same way, which is unusual for a currency normally thought of as a "simple" haven trade:

  • The haven force (bullish): With the Iran war headlines dominating the tape and now into a fifth consecutive night of escalation post-ceasefire-collapse, CHF is one of the two or three currencies markets reach for defensively — alongside USD and, in theory, JPY. Every SNB statement since December 2025 has flagged the same risk explicitly: geopolitical tension is generating appreciation pressure on the franc that the SNB has to actively manage.
  • The zero-rate, low-ammunition force (structurally capping upside, two-sided): Unlike the ECB, which just hiked into the same war, the SNB has held its policy rate at 0% through both the March and June 2026 assessments and is not signalling any near-term move off zero. A central bank with literally no policy rate to defend is a very different animal from one that's actively tightening — the franc's strength right now is coming almost entirely from safe-haven flows and FX-intervention restraint, not from a yield story. That's a fragile kind of strength: it can reverse quickly if risk sentiment improves, and the SNB has openly signalled it is willing to intervene to slow it down.
  • The trade-policy force (idiosyncratic, two-sided): CHF is also carrying a franc-specific overhang that EUR doesn't have in the same way — the unresolved status of the US tariff regime on Swiss goods. Switzerland went from a 39% "reciprocal" tariff in August 2025, to a 15% negotiated framework rate in November 2025, to a flat 10% Section 122 rate since February 2026 that is due to expire around 24 July 2026 — just over a week from this report — with a permanent, legally binding deal still being negotiated. That's a real, dated catalyst that is genuinely unresolved as of today.

The net effect: CHF strength is currently well-supported but for reasons that are largely exogenous to Switzerland itself (war headlines, global risk appetite, US trade politics) rather than endogenous (SNB tightening, a hot economy). That distinction matters enormously for durability — it's the CHF-equivalent of the EUR report's observation that EUR's gains are mostly a dollar story rather than a euro story.

🎙 SCRIPT
Let's talk about the franc, because it's a genuinely different animal from most of the currencies we cover. Switzerland's central bank is sitting at zero percent interest rates — it hasn't moved since June of last year, and it's not really planning to. So when the franc strengthens, it's not because Swiss bonds suddenly pay more, it's because the world is nervous, and money reflexively runs to Switzerland the same way it runs to the dollar or gold. That's the safe-haven mechanic, and with the Iran war still escalating, that mechanic is very much switched on right now. But here's the twist: the Swiss National Bank doesn't love a franc that's too strong — a stronger franc makes Swiss exports more expensive and imports cheaper, which drags down inflation that's already barely above zero. So the SNB has explicitly said it's ready to step into the currency market and lean against a franc that rises too fast. On top of all that, there's a very Swiss-specific wildcard: a temporary US tariff on Swiss goods is set to expire in just over a week, and nobody quite knows yet what replaces it. So you've got safe-haven demand pulling the franc up, a central bank that might lean against that strength, and a trade-policy cliff-edge, all happening at once.

3. SNB Policy Deep Dive

Where rates stand

SNB policy rate: 0.00%, unchanged since the SNB's move to a zero-rate policy in June 2025. The Governing Board held again at both the 19 March 2026 and 18 June 2026 quarterly assessments. Sight deposits are remunerated at the policy rate up to a threshold, with a 0.25 percentage-point discount applied above it.

Next scheduled decision

The SNB holds four in-depth assessments a year — March, June, September, December — each on a Thursday at 9:30 CET with a press conference at 10:00 CET. Following that quarterly cadence, the next assessment is expected around late September 2026 (the exact date is confirmed closer to the meeting on the SNB's website); markets and Swiss Life's house view currently see the policy rate remaining at 0% through at least the end of 2026, with any move higher pushed into 2027.

The conditional inflation forecast

At the June 2026 assessment the SNB put average annual inflation at 0.6% for 2026, 0.6% for 2027, and 0.7% for 2028 — all conditional on the policy rate staying at 0% throughout. The near-term path was revised slightly higher than March (higher commodity prices, higher inflation abroad), but the medium-term picture is virtually unchanged. This is the single clearest guide to the likely rate path: as long as this forecast holds within the SNB's price-stability range, there is no urgency to move.

The FX-intervention nuance — the real policy lever right now

Because the policy rate has almost no room to move, the SNB's de facto active tool is its currency-intervention stance. Through 2025 and into March 2026, statements referred to an "increased willingness" to intervene, framed around the risk of "rapid and excessive appreciation" of the franc. At the June 2026 meeting, SNB Chairman Martin Schlegel notably softened that language to a conditional "if necessary" — a shift he linked to looser monetary conditions following the franc's depreciation since March. That's a genuinely important tell: it means the SNB's tolerance threshold for franc strength moves around with the exchange rate itself, and a renewed leg of war-driven appreciation could quickly flip the language back to more assertive.

The negative-rate tail risk

Because Swiss inflation is already close to zero and could tip toward deflation if the franc appreciates too aggressively (cheaper imports, weaker export competitiveness), a return to negative rates — which prevailed from January 2015 to June 2025 — remains a live, if not base-case, tool in the SNB's kit if franc strength becomes disorderly. This is the clearest asymmetry in the whole CHF picture: the SNB has essentially one credible lever to fight excess strength (intervention, and ultimately negative rates again), and almost no credible lever to fight weakness beyond simply staying on hold.

Forward guidance style

The SNB does not pre-commit and reassesses quarterly; unlike the ECB's more verbose forward guidance, SNB communication is comparatively terse, meaning market moves around each release are often driven as much by the 10:00 CET press conference tone as by the decision itself.

June's Swiss CPI came in at 0.5% year-on-year, down from 0.6% in May and slightly below/in-line with consensus depending on the source. Core inflation (ex food and energy) held at 0.3%, unchanged from May and still among the softest readings since mid-2021. On a month-on-month basis the index was flat. Housing/energy inflation eased to roughly 1.2% y/y from 1.5%, transport eased to around 1.8% from 2.0%, and restaurants/hotels softened to about 0.7% from 1.0% — a broad-based cooling rather than one-off noise. This is a genuinely mild inflation print set against a eurozone reading nearly six times higher (2.8%) in the same month — a reminder of just how differently exposed the Swiss and eurozone economies are to the same global energy shock.

Why this cooling is also fragile, in the other direction from EUR: the SNB's own June forecast has quarterly inflation rising from 0.6% in Q2 2026 to 0.7% in Q3 and 0.8% in Q4 2026 before easing again in H1 2027 — the mirror image of the ECB's near-term profile. If Middle East oil disruption intensifies further, Swiss inflation could push toward the top of its comfort range faster than currently forecast; conversely, a sustained further franc appreciation (haven demand) would import disinflation and could tip the SNB back toward discussing negative rates. Both directions are plausible from here, which is exactly why SNB language has stayed deliberately non-committal.

Rents remain the quiet structural driver

Housing rents in the Swiss CPI are only refreshed quarterly (Feb/May/Aug/Nov survey months) and are closely linked to the policy rate; the current 1.4% y/y rental inflation reading is a lagged echo of last year's rate-cutting cycle and will next update in the August survey — a mechanical, non-discretionary data point worth flagging as a near-term catalyst in its own right (Section 10).

SECO's initial Q1 2026 estimate showed the Swiss economy outperforming expectations, growing 0.5% (later revised to a comparable 0.4% sports-adjusted figure) despite what SECO itself described as "the oil price shock and tariff turmoil" — a notable resilience given Switzerland absorbed both the direct 39%-then-15%-then-10% US tariff whiplash and the broader Middle East energy shock in the same quarter. The industrial sector, led by pharmaceuticals, was the key driver; services growth was more subdued and domestic final demand was described by SECO as outright weak, with private consumption flat and fixed investment slightly negative.

What's actually working: the KOF Barometer's June jump was broad-based — foreign demand indicators, private-consumption indicators, and especially manufacturing production indicators all improved. Manufacturing PMI order books, while decelerating from May's spike, remain solidly above 50. And crucially, the growth story is no longer being told against a backdrop of maximum tariff uncertainty: the move from a 39% to a 10-15% effective US tariff rate over the past year has materially de-risked the export outlook versus where things stood in August 2025.

The two competing 2026 growth forecasts worth knowing about: KOF's own June forecast round actually cut its 2026 GDP growth projection to 0.8% (from 1.0%) and 2027 to 1.5% (from 1.7%), explicitly citing the US-Israel war on Iran and higher oil prices as bigger drags than previously assumed — even as the same institute's economists separately flagged that Switzerland's growth outlook would exceed 1% if the tariff relief holds. Meanwhile, private-bank house views (e.g. Lombard Odier) went the other way, upgrading 2026 GDP to 1.2% on the back of the November tariff-reduction framework. The SNB's own June baseline sits in between, at "around 1%" for 2026 and "around 1.5%" for 2027. The honest takeaway: growth forecasts for Switzerland are currently being pulled in opposite directions by the same two shocks (war vs. tariff relief) that are also driving the currency — there is no clean consensus right now.

6. The US Tariff Overhang — CHF's Idiosyncratic Risk Factor

How we got here

Switzerland was hit with a 39% "reciprocal" US tariff from 7 August 2025 — the highest rate applied to any developed economy, hitting roughly two-thirds of Swiss goods exports. A framework agreement announced 14 November 2025 cut this to 15% (parity with the EU) in exchange for a Swiss pledge to invest at least $200bn in the US through 2028 ($67bn in 2026 alone), tariff-rate quotas on US poultry/beef/bison, and various non-tariff-barrier concessions. Following the US Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling that struck down the IEEPA legal basis for the original tariffs, the applicable rate on Swiss goods dropped further to a flat 10% under Section 122 of the US Trade Act, in place since 24 February 2026.

The near-term cliff-edge

That Section 122 tariff is time-limited to 150 days and is due to expire around 24 July 2026 — just over a week after this report, and the same day as the ECB's next decision. A Court of International Trade ruling in May 2026 found even the Section 122 tariff unlawful, though a Federal Circuit stay is keeping it in force pending appeal. In parallel, Section 232 sectoral tariffs remain in force on steel (50%), aluminium (50%), and — since April 2026 — pharmaceuticals (up to 15%, with company-specific exemptions already secured by Roche and Novartis).

Where the permanent deal stands

As of 8 July 2026, Switzerland's top trade official (SECO director Helene Budliger Artieda) described a "good chance" of locking in a lasting 15% rate — parity with the EU — but stressed talks remain live and unresolved, particularly around an ongoing US Section 301 investigation that Washington has so far refused to delink from the broader negotiation. Notably, a domestic Swiss opinion poll found 63% of respondents viewed the original framework deal as unfavourable to Switzerland — a reminder that even a "resolved" outcome carries some domestic political friction.

Why this matters for CHF specifically: this is a genuine, dated, franc-specific event risk that has no real EUR equivalent (the EU's 15% rate is already locked in). A clean rollover or finalization of the 15% permanent rate around 24 July would remove a real growth-downside tail for Swiss exporters; a lapse back toward higher, uncertain tariffs — even temporarily, while courts and negotiators sort things out — would be a fresh negative growth shock layered directly on top of the war-driven one, and is exactly the kind of headline that could tip KOF/manufacturing PMI back down.

8. Positioning & Market Structure Notes

  • CHF is one of the two or three "primary" war-haven currencies — unlike EUR, which sits closer to a risk-and-rates proxy exposed to the energy shock as a cost. This gives CHF a cleaner, more directly positive reaction function to further Middle East escalation than most G10 currencies, which is the core reason its scorecard skews firmer than the eurozone's despite an objectively less exciting central bank.
  • The SNB is a genuine two-way actor in FX, unusual among G10 central banks. Most central banks talk about the exchange rate as a side-effect of policy; the SNB explicitly manages it as a policy target in its own right, and has a multi-decade history of large-scale, sometimes unannounced, FX intervention (most famously the 2011-2015 EUR/CHF floor). Any sharp, disorderly CHF appreciation carries real intervention-headline risk that doesn't exist to the same degree for EUR, GBP, or AUD.
  • The zero-rate structural carry drag means CHF longs typically need a risk-off or franc-specific catalyst to work — in calm, risk-on markets, CHF tends to bleed lower against higher-yielding currencies as carry trades re-engage; this is the persistent tension underneath the near-term haven bid.
  • The tariff deadline (~24 July) is a genuinely under-discussed, dated catalyst relative to how much attention it's getting versus the ECB/Iran-war headlines — worth flagging precisely because it's easy to overlook next to the bigger macro stories.

11. Key Risks That Could Invalidate This Analysis

  • A durable Iran ceasefire would unwind the haven bid quickly — this is the single biggest swing factor for CHF, larger in relative terms than the equivalent risk for EUR, precisely because so much of CHF's recent strength is haven-flow-dependent rather than fundamentals-dependent.
  • A disorderly further franc appreciation could trigger actual SNB FX intervention (selling CHF, buying foreign currency) rather than just rhetoric — a real, event-risk catalyst that could cap or reverse CHF gains abruptly and without much warning, given the SNB's history of surprise action.
  • A bad outcome on the ~24 July tariff deadline — either a lapse back to a higher/more punitive rate, or a prolonged stalemate — would be a fresh, franc-specific growth shock layered on top of the war-driven uncertainty already in the data.
  • A shift in SNB language toward discussing negative rates — currently not the base case, but the SNB has done this before (2015-2025) and would do so again if disinflation risk from franc strength combined with a genuine growth slowdown; watch the September conditional inflation forecast closely for early signs.
  • Further Section 232/301 sectoral tariff escalation on pharmaceuticals — Switzerland's single largest export sector; renewed threats here (beyond Roche/Novartis's existing three-year shields) would hit the growth outlook disproportionately given pharma's outsized role in Q1's GDP resilience.
  • A sharp global risk-on episode independent of the war (e.g. an unexpected resolution to US-China trade tensions, a strong run of US data) could pull carry flows out of zero-yielding CHF even with the war unresolved — CHF's rate disadvantage doesn't disappear just because the haven bid is currently overriding it.
  • US-side surprises dominating USD/CHF — as with EUR/USD, a reversal in recent US-data softness (e.g. a hot US CPI print) could move USD/CHF independent of anything happening in Switzerland.

Overall Presumption

CHF today is best understood as a currency whose recent strength is real but disproportionately borrowed from outside its own borders — a war it isn't party to, and a US administration's tariff decisions it doesn't fully control. Underneath that borrowed strength sits a central bank with almost no conventional policy room left, which makes CHF's risk profile genuinely two-sided in a way that's easy to overlook when the haven narrative dominates the headlines: a ceasefire could unwind the bid as quickly as it built, and the SNB has both the tools and the stated willingness to lean against further appreciation if it goes too far, too fast. The next real tests arrive on a tight timeline: the US tariff deadline around 24 July, Swiss CPI and KOF data through late July/August, and the SNB's own updated forecast in September. Until those land, CHF should be treated as a haven-and-headline currency whose direction depends more on decisions made in Washington and Tehran than on anything the SNB itself is likely to do.
