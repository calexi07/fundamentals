CAD Deep-Dive Fundamental Report

As of: 2026-07-16  |  Scope: Single-currency deep dive on CAD — monetary policy, inflation, growth, trade/fiscal risk, positioning, cross-by-cross bias, catalysts & scenario tree  |  Companion piece to: Daily Global Macro Strategy Report

One-line view: CAD is a genuine two-way commodity-and-trade currency right now — a stabilizing labour market and a central bank that just turned tactically hawkish (Macklem explicitly floated future hikes) are pulling one way, while a stalled CUSMA renewal, a structurally tariff-damaged manufacturing sector, and a currency that is only loosely riding this month's oil rally are pulling the other. The Bank of Canada held rates at 2.25% on 15 July for a sixth straight decision, but the tone shifted meaningfully hawkish relative to prior holds — this is the single most important development of the week for CAD.

1. What's Actually Driving CAD Right Now — The Core Tension

CAD is being pulled by three forces simultaneously, which is unusual — most currencies are fighting a two-way battle, but the loonie right now has a monetary-policy leg, a trade-policy leg, and a commodity leg all moving at once, and they are not all pointing the same direction:

  • The hawkish-turn force: The Bank of Canada held its policy rate at 2.25% on 15 July 2026 — the sixth consecutive hold — but Governor Macklem's press conference language was the most hawkish-leaning of the cycle. He acknowledged war-driven oil prices are pushing up gasoline costs and said that if oil stays elevated for long enough, the Bank may well need to raise rates, explicitly calling this "not our base case, but... a serious risk." The Bank also noted growth has resumed more strongly than expected, with Q2 GDP tracking near 2.5% annualized after a choppy first quarter — a much stronger growth signal than markets had priced in going into the decision.
  • The trade-policy drag force: The 1 July 2026 CUSMA joint review passed without the US agreeing to the 16-year extension. The deal doesn't expire until 2036 and existing tariff exemptions remain in place for now, but the review has shifted into an annual, open-ended renegotiation process with no fixed resolution date — a state of prolonged ambiguity rather than either a clean win or a clean loss. Manufacturing employment has fallen by roughly 61,000 jobs since tariff uncertainty began in January 2025, and the Bank of Canada's own projections see 2026 GDP finishing around 1.5% below its pre-tariff trajectory.
  • The oil/commodity force: Canada is a major net oil exporter, and WTI has rallied to roughly $79-80 (Brent near $83) on the back of renewed US-Iran conflict escalation, US strikes near the Strait of Hormuz, and a reinstated naval blockade threatening shipping — normally a tailwind for CAD via the terms-of-trade channel. But this rally is happening at the same time as broad USD strength and a widening short-term Canada-US yield disadvantage, which is why CAD has only partially captured the "petrocurrency" benefit this cycle.

Net result: USD/CAD sits near 1.40, off its worst levels (a 15-month CAD low near 1.4235 hit in late June) but still historically weak, up roughly 2.3% against the dollar over the past 12 months even after the recent bounce. The bounce itself is mostly a domestic-data and BoC-tone story — stronger June jobs data and the hawkish hold — rather than CAD benefiting cleanly from the oil rally, which is the key nuance to track from here.

But because the US dollar has also been broadly strong and short-term rate spreads still favor the US, Canada hasn't captured the full benefit of that oil rally. So you've got three forces at once: a newly hawkish central bank, an unresolved trade cloud, and an oil tailwind that isn't fully translating — that's why the currency looks calm on the surface but is actually being pulled in three directions underneath.

3. Bank of Canada Policy Deep Dive

Where rates stand

Overnight rate target: 2.25%, unchanged since the last cut. Bank Rate 2.50%, deposit rate 2.20%. This was the Bank's fifth scheduled decision of 2026 and the sixth consecutive hold, meaning the BoC has now been on pause for roughly a full easing-cycle pause window while it assesses the war-driven inflation shock and the tariff drag simultaneously.

Next decision date

The next scheduled Governing Council rate announcement is Wednesday, 2 September 2026. The Bank meets eight times a year on pre-set dates, with the Monetary Policy Report published alongside the January, April, July and October decisions — meaning the July MPR just released is the most current full projection set available.

What the 15 July statement actually said

The Bank's language was notably two-sided: it said Canada's economy is showing signs of improvement, with growth picking up and inflation projected to ease gradually from its recent spike, while also flagging that global economic prospects have been dented by higher oil prices stemming from the Middle East conflict. Governing Council judged the current policy rate remains appropriate for now, while stating it is prepared to adjust monetary policy as needed and remains committed to preventing higher energy costs from becoming persistent, broad-based inflation.

The Macklem hike-risk comment — the real signal

In the press conference, Macklem was unusually direct about the hike scenario: he said that if oil prices go higher and stay higher, the likelihood of pass-through into broader prices increases, and that this progression from "broadening" to "persistence" is exactly the trigger that would force a rate hike. He explicitly called a hike "not our base case, but... a serious risk" — language markets read as more hawkish than any BoC communication since the cutting cycle began in 2024.

The growth upgrade nobody expected

The Bank now projects global GDP growth slowing to 2.75% in 2026, mostly due to the Middle East conflict, before recovering toward 3.25% in 2027-28 — but domestically, it flagged that growth has clearly resumed in Q2 2026, with an estimated 2.5% pace, a sharp improvement after Canada's GDP data over the past year was described as "choppy" amid tariff adjustment, high uncertainty, and slower population growth. AI infrastructure build-out was cited as a supporting factor for global activity broadly.

Labour slack still the dovish anchor

The unemployment rate was 6.5% in June and has hovered in a 6.5%-7.0% range since late 2024 — a level the Bank still characterizes as reflecting ongoing economic slack, which is the main reason a hike remains a tail scenario rather than the base case despite Macklem's comments.

May's headline print rose to 3.2% from 2.8% in April, ahead of consensus and the fastest pace since December 2023, driven overwhelmingly by a 33.2% year-over-year jump in gasoline prices and a broader 9% rise in energy costs as the Middle East war disrupted energy exports. With WTI up further into the high-$70s/low-$80s on the latest wave of US-Iran escalation and Strait of Hormuz tensions, June and July CPI prints are at real risk of pushing headline inflation still further above 3%, even if core stays anchored. June's headline jobs beat masks a composition problem worth flagging clearly: gains were concentrated in part-time work, accommodation and food services, and wholesale/retail trade — sectors economists explicitly linked to a temporary boost from Canada hosting FIFA World Cup matches in June. 6. Trade & Fiscal Risk — The CUSMA Overhang

The July 1 review — what actually happened

Under Article 34.7 of the agreement, Canada, the US and Mexico were required to hold a joint six-year review on 1 July 2026 and choose between a clean 16-year extension (to 2042) or moving into annual reviews with no fixed extension date. Canada and Mexico had both formally signalled intent to renew; the United States declined to commit to the 16-year extension. CUSMA does not expire until 2036 and current tariff treatment is unchanged for now — roughly 90% of Canadian exports to the US still enter duty-free under existing CUSMA compliance — but the deal has effectively shifted into an open-ended, year-by-year negotiation with no clear end point, which multiple trade analysts expect to run well beyond the US midterms and possibly into 2027.

What's actually at stake if it unravels further

RBC Economics estimates that without CUSMA protections, Canada's average effective US tariff rate could roughly double, from about 3.2% to 6.6% — still low by the standards of major US trading partners, but a real shock to CUSMA-exempt sectors like autos, machinery, plastics, aluminum and wood products, several of which are already exposed to separate Section 232 sectoral tariffs on steel, aluminum and autos. The Bank of Canada's own base-case projection assumes CUSMA effectively gets extended in substance even under the annual-review structure; a genuinely adversarial renegotiation is treated as a downside risk scenario, not the central forecast.

The offsetting positive: energy infrastructure and diversification

On 2 July, the federal government approved a new pipeline capable of shipping up to one million barrels per day of Alberta bitumen to British Columbia for export to Asian markets — part of a broader push, alongside new defence-sector reindustrialization spending, to diversify Canada's trade exposure away from near-total US dependence. The US share of Canadian exports has already fallen to roughly 71.7%, its lowest since the early 1980s, as exporters have found other markets even as goods exports to the US itself declined. This is a genuine medium-term positive for FDI inflows and CAD that sits alongside the CUSMA overhang rather than cancelling it out.

8. Positioning & Market Structure Notes

  • CAD is lagging its own commodity beta. The textbook relationship — oil up, CAD up — has weakened this cycle because broad USD strength and a widening short-term Canada-US yield gap are offsetting the terms-of-trade tailwind. Watch for this relationship to reassert itself if oil escalation continues while US data softens; that combination would be the cleanest bullish-CAD setup available.
  • The World Cup effect is a genuine positioning trap. June's stronger-than-expected jobs number was flagged by multiple bank economists as partly a tournament-hosting effect concentrated in food service, retail and part-time work. A soft July or August jobs report as that effect fades would not necessarily represent fresh deterioration, but headline algos and less careful positioning may treat it that way — a real source of potential CAD volatility around the next two labour-force releases.
  • CUSMA headline risk is now a standing, not episodic, risk. Because the review has moved into an open-ended annual process rather than resolving on a single date, CAD is now exposed to trade headlines on an ongoing basis rather than around one clean binary event — this argues for somewhat elevated baseline CAD volatility through the rest of 2026 relative to a normal year.
  • Rate-differential-driven flows remain the dominant medium-term anchor. Bank desks (e.g., National Bank's FX strategy team) continue to flag Canada's unfavourable two-year yield spread versus the US as a structural CAD headwind independent of the day-to-day news flow; a Fed hike later in 2026, if it materializes, would widen this further and is arguably a bigger swing factor for USD/CAD than most Canada-specific data surprises.

11. Key Risks That Could Invalidate This Analysis

  • A June CPI surprise in either direction — a hot print pulls forward hike pricing fast given Macklem's comments; a soft print (especially if core also softens) would undercut the entire hawkish-repricing thesis just four days after this report.
  • A durable Iran ceasefire would remove CAD's main current tailwind (the oil rally) while simultaneously easing the inflation pressure that's keeping the BoC's hike option alive — a genuinely ambiguous net effect that needs watching closely rather than assumed to be bearish or bullish.
  • Further war escalation / a full Hormuz shipping disruption could push oil sharply higher, which is bullish for Canada's terms of trade but could also hit global growth and risk appetite hard enough to trigger a broader flight from commodity currencies — the classic case where the "petrocurrency" trade breaks down under acute stress.
  • A hard CUSMA breakdown or fresh punitive tariff wave — since the trade relationship is now in an open-ended negotiation with no scheduled resolution, this risk has no natural expiry date and could resurface at any point without the usual calendar warning.
  • The World Cup labour-market payback — if July/August jobs data reveals June's strength was concentrated, temporary, and now reversing, that would materially change the growth narrative the BoC leaned on for its 15 July hawkish tone.
  • A Fed hike Canada doesn't match — would widen the already-unfavorable Canada-US rate differential and could dominate CAD price action over Canada-specific news, especially in a base-case, range-bound scenario.
  • Manufacturing deterioration accelerating — the -61,000 job trend since January 2025 is the clearest real-economy signature of the tariff dispute; a sharp acceleration would undercut the "growth is improving" narrative even if headline GDP stays positive.

Overall Presumption

CAD today is a currency in an unusually rare setup: a central bank that just, for the first time this cycle, openly floated the possibility of a hike, sitting on top of genuinely improving growth data, with a real (if only partially captured) oil tailwind — but all of it sitting alongside a trade relationship that just entered a more uncertain, open-ended phase rather than getting resolved. None of the three forces (policy, growth, trade) has fully resolved in either direction, which is why conviction should stay moderate. The next real test arrives fast: June CPI on 20 July will either validate or undercut the BoC's new hawkish tone within days of this report, followed by Q2 GDP, the July jobs report, and the ongoing, undated flow of CUSMA headlines. Until the CPI print lands, CAD is a headline-and-differential currency, not a clean trend.
