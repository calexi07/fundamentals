5. Best Setups to Watch — Fundamental View

Ranked by conviction. These are fundamental theses only — pair them with the technical SMC analysis before sizing any actual entry.

1. EURUSD — Short Highest conviction

Started: Fundamentally, this thesis has been building since the ECB's June hike failed to stop EUR's slide (EUR/USD has lost ~1.69% over the past month, hitting fresh ~1-year lows on 13 July); the current leg accelerated with the Hormuz re-escalation from ~10 July onward. Not a single-day signal — this is a multi-week structural divergence.

The cleanest divergence in the entire set: EUR is the structurally weakest currency of the eight (hiked in June yet still lost ground, weak growth, exposed to energy shock) against USD, which — even after today's dovish CPI wobble — retains the broadest support base (safe-haven flows from Hormuz, still-firm labor and inflation backdrop relative to peers). Already confirmed independently by the SMC technical picture built earlier this week, which is unusual and adds real weight.

Invalidated if: (a) A confirmed Hormuz ceasefire/de-escalation — would remove the USD haven bid faster than it fixes EUR's underlying weakness, but a sustained daily close back above ~1.1480 (the Daily supply zone identified in the SMC EURUSD analysis) would be the technical confirmation to watch; (b) the ECB delivers a clearly hawkish surprise at the 23 July meeting (e.g., explicit guidance toward back-to-back hikes) while the Fed turns dovish at 28-29 July FOMC — that combination would flip the rate-differential argument against this thesis specifically, as opposed to just causing a pause.

2. USDJPY — Long, reduced size High conviction, event-capped

Started: The structural rate-gap thesis has been intact since the BoJ's 16-17 June hike to 1.00% failed to meaningfully close the gap with the Fed's 3.50-3.75% — USD/JPY has been grinding toward ¥160 since late June/early July. This is a durable, months-old structural theme, not a fresh signal.

The purest yield-differential trade available (Fed 3.50–3.75% vs. BoJ 1.00% — one of the widest gaps among majors even after this year's BoJ hikes). The case is simple and durable as long as the rate gap persists. The catch is entirely event risk, not fundamentals: USD/JPY is sitting near the ¥160 level that already triggered an ¥11.7 trillion intervention in May, and the BoJ's 30–31 July meeting could surprise hawkish. This is a good trade to run at reduced size or with a defined risk budget around that date, not a "set and forget" position.

Invalidated if: (a) Fresh MoF/BoJ FX intervention — has happened once already this year (May, ~¥11.7tn) at similar levels, so a repeat is a real, not theoretical, risk with essentially no advance warning; (b) a hawkish surprise at the 30-31 July BoJ meeting (e.g., a larger-than-25bp hike or explicit guidance toward faster tightening) would directly narrow the rate gap this thesis depends on; (c) a sharp, sustained global risk-off shock — JPY has historically strengthened sharply in acute crises via carry-trade unwinding, which would work against this position even though the current Hormuz-driven episode hasn't triggered that dynamic yet.

3. EURAUD — Short Good conviction

Started: This is more of a "watch and build" idea than an active, dated thesis — it follows directly from the EUR-weakness case above but hasn't been running as a distinct tracked position. Treat it as starting now, from today's scores, rather than backdated.

Pairs the single weakest currency (EUR) against the highest absolute yielder (AUD, 4.35%). The main noise factor is that AUD itself is fighting a risk-off headwind from the Hormuz conflict and soft China demand data — but that headwind affects the AUD leg on both sides of most AUD crosses, whereas EUR's weakness is closer to unconditional. Worth watching for confirmation around the 11 August RBA decision, which is the clearest near-term catalyst for the AUD leg specifically.

Invalidated if: (a) The RBA holds on 11 August with dovish language (opens the door to cuts) — would remove AUD's main yield advantage; (b) a sharp global risk-off escalation that hits AUD harder than EUR (plausible given AUD's higher-beta status) would work against the short even if EUR stays weak in absolute terms; (c) any sign the ECB is turning more aggressively hawkish than the single priced September hike.

4. EURGBP — Short, moderate conviction Moderate

Started: New idea as of this report — GBP's political overhang (Starmer's June resignation) and EUR's ongoing weakness have coexisted for weeks, but this is the first time the two are being framed together as a specific pair thesis.

Two currencies each carrying their own baggage — EUR's structural weakness against GBP's political overhang (post-Starmer leadership vacuum) — but GBP's hawkish-leaning BoE hold (7–2 vote) gives it a modest edge on the monetary-policy dimension specifically. Less clean than EURUSD or EURAUD because GBP's own path is genuinely two-sided; treat this as a lower-size expression of the same "EUR is weak" thesis rather than a standalone high-conviction idea.

Invalidated if: (a) UK political risk escalates further (e.g., a fiscal-credibility scare echoing the 2022 Truss episode) — would hit GBP harder than EUR and flip this thesis; (b) the BoE turns dovish at the 30 July decision (a straight hold with soft language, rather than the current hawkish-leaning hold with dissents wanting a hike); (c) weak UK data (retail sales already missed in June) continues to accumulate into a genuine growth scare.

5. Gold — Tactical long on dips toward $3,960–4,000 Moderate, wide range

Started: This support level has been defended multiple times since gold's correction began after the January 2026 all-time high (~$5,595-5,600); the current test is the most recent in that pattern, active as of 13-14 July when gold briefly dipped below $4,000 before rebounding. It's an ongoing, recurring range thesis rather than a single dated entry.

Not a directional conviction call so much as a range/dip-buying thesis: gold has defended the $3,960–4,000 support level multiple times in 2026, backed by continuing central bank accumulation (PBoC's 20th straight month of buying in June) even as hawkish Fed repricing caps the upside. The asymmetry favors buying weakness over chasing strength — a further Hormuz escalation or any dovish Fed surprise would be the catalysts for a real breakout above the $4,300 resistance area, while a sustained de-escalation is the main risk to the downside case.

Invalidated if: A decisive, sustained daily close below $3,960 (not just an intraday dip) — per technical desks, that would open a much deeper move, with some chartists citing a head-and-shoulders target as low as $2,575-2,750. That would only be likely on a combination of a real Hormuz resolution AND a hawkish Fed surprise happening together.

6. CADJPY — Long, post-BoC confirmation only Speculative until today's data

Started: Not started — this is explicitly a watchlist idea pending today's 9:45 ET Bank of Canada decision and Monetary Policy Report tone. Do not treat as active until that data is in.

An interesting idea rather than a current conviction call: CAD is the one currency in the set structurally helped by the same oil spike that's driving broader risk-off, while JPY remains a funding currency on the rate gap. The combination could make CADJPY an efficient way to express "oil stays elevated" without taking on direct Middle East equity exposure. Needs today's Bank of Canada tone (not just the widely-expected hold itself) before this becomes more than a watchlist idea.

Invalidated if: (a) The BoC MPR tone is more dovish than expected (e.g., openly worried about the reported technical recession and weak ~6.5-7% unemployment) — would undercut the CAD side of this idea even with the oil tailwind; (b) a Hormuz de-escalation reverses the oil rally, removing CAD's main current advantage; (c) a hawkish BoJ surprise at 30-31 July.

7. DAX — Buy dips into the energy-driven pullback Moderate, structural

Started: The pullback itself began right after the DAX's record close of ~25,779-25,827 in the week of 6 July, accelerating with the Hormuz re-escalation from ~10 July onward — so this dip-buy thesis has been live for roughly a week as of this report.

The pullback from the early-July record high (~25,800) toward ~25,000 looks driven by Europe's energy-price sensitivity to the Hormuz shock rather than by any deterioration in the index's own fundamentals — industrials/financials-heavy composition, cheaper valuations than US tech, and a genuine rotation-into-value tailwind are all still in place. A reasonable dip-buy thesis for anyone already constructive on European equities, with the obvious caveat that a further oil spike would extend the pullback before any of those structural positives reassert themselves.

Invalidated if: A sustained break below the prior consolidation range (the CMC Markets 2026 outlook flags ~22,900-24,400 as the multi-month range the DAX broke out of before the July rally) would suggest the structural bull case itself is under threat, not just a tactical pullback. Also watch Q2 earnings from key constituents (Volkswagen 24 July, Rheinmetall 8 August) for company-specific risk that could compound the macro pullback.

6. Key Risks That Could Invalidate This Analysis

  • Sudden Hormuz resolution or fresh escalation — the single largest swing factor across nearly every instrument in this report.
  • Bank of Canada surprise (today) — consensus is overwhelmingly for a hold, but the Monetary Policy Report tone could move CAD across the board.
  • BoJ intervention or hawkish surprise (30-31 July) — could snap JPY sharply stronger with little warning given the ¥11.7tn already spent this year.
  • SNB intervention against CHF strength — explicitly flagged as live by the SNB itself.
  • UK political developments — unresolved leadership situation post-Starmer.
  • AI-capex disappointment in tech earnings — could hit US100/Nasdaq disproportionately given how much of S&P earnings growth is concentrated in that sector this quarter.
  • China demand data — June oil imports fell 41% YoY, a yellow flag for AUD-relevant global demand.

Overall Presumption

The macro picture from yesterday still holds structurally, but today's cooler CPI print is a reminder of how fast the "USD strength" narrative can wobble even while the underlying safe-haven and relative-fundamental case stays intact — it's a genuine two-way market right now, not a one-way freight train. EURUSD remains the cleanest, most fundamentally-confirmed short in the set. USDJPY is the next-cleanest idea on pure yield differential but carries real intervention/event risk into the 30–31 July BoJ meeting. The commodity bloc (AUD, NZD, CAD) is currently a tug-of-war between yield and risk-aversion with no dominant winner, and CAD specifically has a live, near-certain-outcome-but-still-market-moving event today. Gold and the equity indices are all, in their own way, hostage to the same Hormuz/Fed tension driving the FX picture — Gold torn between haven demand and rate expectations, US100 torn between AI-earnings validation and capex doubts, and DAX torn between energy-price sensitivity and a genuine rotation/value tailwind. Nothing here should be treated as settled; the event calendar (BoC today, Warsh follow-through, ECB 23 July, FOMC 28-29 July, BoE and BoJ both 30-31 July, RBA 11 August) is a list of specific moments where this entire picture could shift again.
